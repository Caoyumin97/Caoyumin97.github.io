## How It Started

Over the past few months, I built two projects purely for my own use — a personal ledger with Claude Code and a small quantitative trading application with OpenCode. Neither project was particularly complex, but after finishing them, my understanding of what "software" means shifted in subtle but important ways.

## The Ledger: From "I Want This" to "It Works" in One Evening

I've never been satisfied with the budgeting apps on the market. They're either bloated with features, organize categories in ways that don't match my habits, or raise privacy concerns. In the past, that dissatisfaction was something I just lived with — the cost of building my own was too high.

With Claude Code, I experienced for the first time what it feels like to describe what you want in natural language and watch the code grow into existence. I told it the categorization system I wanted, the statistical views I cared about, the data storage approach I preferred, and it built everything step by step.

The process wasn't perfect, of course. The Agent had a tendency to over-engineer — I just wanted simple SQLite local storage, and it insisted on adding an ORM layer; I wanted a plain CLI, and it kept trying to scaffold a Web UI. But these were all correctable. More importantly, **the cost of correction was negligible** — one sentence in natural language like "drop that, keep it simple" and it adjusted.

The final ledger is organized entirely according to my mental model: categories match my habits, statistics track what I actually care about, and the data lives on my own machine. That sense of "perfect fit" is something no general-purpose software can deliver.

## The Quant App: The Agent's Ceiling and the Engineer's Value

The quantitative trading application was more challenging. It involved data ingestion, strategy backtesting, risk management, and live trading integration — each with its own pitfalls.

This project showed me where the Agent's capability boundary lies. **At the "scaffolding" level, Agents are remarkably efficient.** Data pipelines, backtesting frameworks, basic strategy templates — this kind of highly structured work, Agents handle quickly and well.

But when it came to the details of strategy logic, the Agent's value shifted from "a tool that writes strategies for you" to "an excellent sparring partner." Because a strategy is ultimately an expression of market understanding, and that understanding can't be simply encoded as a prompt. I found my most productive workflow was using the Agent to quickly validate ideas — "write me a mean-reversion strategy based on X and run a backtest over the past three years" — then adjusting direction based on the results.

The stylistic difference between OpenCode and Claude Code was also interesting. Claude Code is more "proactive" — it anticipates your needs and offers suggestions ahead of time. OpenCode is more "faithful" — it follows your instructions precisely. I use both, switching depending on the context.

## Will the Shape of Software Fundamentally Change?

After completing these two projects, I started seriously considering a question: **If anyone can use AI to easily build software perfectly tailored to their own needs, how will the very nature of "software" change?**

### The Technical Lens: From Standardization to Personalization

The history of software engineering is, in some sense, a history of standardization. We build general-purpose software, SaaS products, platforms — all driven by the core logic of "serve the most people with a standardized solution." This was the inevitable choice when software development was expensive: standardization was the only economically viable path.

But AI Agents are pushing the marginal cost of software development toward near-zero. When development cost is no longer the bottleneck, "why settle for a generic solution?" becomes a reasonable question.

I can imagine a future where **many of the needs currently served by apps in the App Store are instead met by personalized tools generated by the user's own Agent.** No downloads, no sign-ups, no adapting to someone else's design decisions — the Agent builds a tool that belongs only to you, shaped by your needs and habits.

Of course, this doesn't mean all software will disappear. Complex infrastructure (databases, operating systems, cloud services), platforms with strong network effects (social media, e-commerce), and systems with stringent security requirements (finance, healthcare) — these will still need professional teams to build and maintain. But the "middle layer" — utility apps, productivity tools, personal management apps — will undergo a massive wave of decentralization.

### The Philosophical Lens: Tools That Fit Like an Extension of Yourself

Heidegger had a concept called "readiness-to-hand" (Zuhandenheit) — the idea that when a tool perfectly fits your usage habits, you "forget" its existence and it becomes an extension of your body. A good hammer in a carpenter's hands is "transparent" — the carpenter doesn't think about the hammer itself; they think about nails and wood.

Most software today is not "ready-to-hand." We spend enormous amounts of time "learning to use" rather than simply "using." Every time we switch apps, we re-adapt. This isn't because designers are incompetent — it's the fundamental contradiction of general-purpose software: it must serve everyone, so it can never perfectly fit anyone.

AI-generated personalized tools may, for the first time, make software truly "ready-to-hand." Because it's built according to your way of thinking — there's no adaptation phase. I felt this when using my own ledger — it doesn't feel like "software"; it feels like a natural extension of myself.

This is a profound shift. **When a tool is no longer an external object you must adapt to, but an extension that conforms entirely to you, the relationship between humans and technology changes.**

### The Social Lens: A New Inequality?

But I have to be honest about the other side as well.

The prerequisite for building personalized tools with AI Agents is: you know what you want, you can describe your needs clearly, and you can judge whether the Agent's output is correct. These three abilities are not evenly distributed.

An engineer using Claude Code and someone with no technical background using the same tool will get vastly different results. Not because the tool has a high barrier to entry, but because "knowing what you want" and "being able to judge output quality" are themselves forms of competence.

This could create a new digital divide — not "can you use a computer" but "can you effectively direct an AI." Some people will end up with highly customized toolsets that fit them perfectly; others will continue using generic, good-enough solutions.

Education needs to catch up. **The most important literacy of the future may not be programming, but "requirements articulation" — the ability to turn a vague feeling into a clear description, to judge which of several Agent outputs is better, to iterate toward what you truly want.**

## Coming Back to the Personal

To close, I want to return to some more personal reflections.

Building projects with AI Agents brought back the excitement I felt when I first learned to code — that feeling of "wait, I can make a machine do this?" The instant feedback of turning an idea into reality, the process of iterating closer and closer to the ideal — that's the pure joy of creation.

The difference is that the path from idea to reality is now much shorter. What might have taken weeks before can now produce an MVP in a single evening. This compression isn't just an efficiency gain — it fundamentally changes how you think about problems. You become more willing to experiment, more willing to build those small things that previously seemed "not worth turning into a real project."

Perhaps this is AI Agents' greatest gift to individuals: **they lower the barrier to creation, giving more ideas a chance to be realized, transforming "building it yourself" from an activity requiring specialized skills into something everyday and natural.**

The future of software may not be more apps — but more people building just-right tools for themselves. That doesn't sound like a revolution, but it might be one.
